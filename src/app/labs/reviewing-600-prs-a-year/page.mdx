# Reviewing 600+ PRs a Year: the patterns I actually use

Over the last twelve months I reviewed **more than 600 pull requests** — more than I authored. That ratio has held for several years now, and the patterns below are what I actually reach for on a Tuesday afternoon when someone drops another PR into my queue.

Nothing here is theory. It's what has survived contact with real teams — small consultancies, a product startup, and cross-functional product pods.

## The 30-second first pass

Every PR gets 30 seconds before I decide what to do with it. Three questions, in order:

1. **What is this trying to change?** — read the title and description. If I can't answer this from the description alone, that's already the first comment.
2. **How big is it?** — files changed, LOC, number of commits. If it's over ~500 LOC, I mentally pre-commit more time.
3. **Am I the right reviewer?** — if not (wrong domain, better reviewer nearby), I re-request review and drop out. Silent lurking is not helpful.

Only then do I look at the diff. About one PR in five gets stopped at step 1 with "help me understand what changed here" — every time it saves me from misreading the code by projecting the wrong intent onto it.

## Triage: what makes a PR merge in under an hour

The PRs that fly through review share a small set of properties:

- **One idea per PR.** If I see a refactor commit mixed with a feature commit, I'll ask to split. Not because rules — because I read them separately anyway.
- **Explicit "why" in the description.** Not "fixes X" — "we need X because Y broke Z."
- **Small.** Under 200 LOC merges roughly 5× faster than 500+ in my data. This isn't a policy, it's physics of attention.
- **A comment explaining the tricky bit.** If there's a subtle piece, the author has already told me where it is.

I've noticed: authors who reliably ship small, well-described PRs get the fastest reviews from me. Not favouritism — cost of context.

## The "do it as a diff comment" rule

The single technique that made me a faster reviewer is this: **suggest changes as inline diff suggestions** rather than descriptions.

Instead of:

> Consider extracting the `formatDate` helper — the same logic is in three other files.

Write:

```suggestion
import { formatDate } from '@/lib/format';
```

...as a GitHub suggestion block on the offending line. Author clicks "commit suggestion," we're done. What would have been a 5-minute back-and-forth is 30 seconds.

I use it for:

- Renames (extract this variable / method / component)
- Import fixes
- Small refactors under 5 lines
- Type annotations

For anything larger I still write prose. But the small stuff — which is 60% of what I comment on — goes as a suggestion.

## When to comment vs pair

I have a simple rule: if I'd need three comments in a row to explain something, it's a pairing session.

Signals I've hit the "should have paired" wall:

- I've written a paragraph and I'm still not sure the author will understand the shape of what I want.
- My comments are turning into a review of the entire design.
- I've asked "why did you...?" more than twice.
- The author's replies are getting shorter and less confident.

At that point I stop typing and say **"can I have 15 minutes with you today?"** — usually we knock it out and I approve during the call. The PR merges same day instead of dragging across a week.

Corollary: if a PR is at four rounds of comments and still not converging, that's evidence I should have paired at round two.

## Reviewing above your level

The most valuable reviews I've done have been on code where I wasn't the domain expert. Two things I've learned:

1. **You can review intent even when you can't review implementation.** "This function's name doesn't match what it does" is a valid comment even when you can't judge whether the algorithm is optimal.
2. **Confusion is signal.** If I have to reread a block three times to follow it, I flag it. Future-me won't have context either. The author is close to the code and blind to it; my confusion is the artefact worth surfacing.

Junior reviewers often skip PRs they don't fully understand. That's a mistake. Your outside eyes are exactly what the PR needs.

## How the AI bot fits in

We use an AI code reviewer that comments on every PR. It's useful and it's not a threat to human review — the two roles are different:

- **The bot catches** style violations, obvious null-safety, forgotten error handling, test coverage gaps. It's good at surface-level things and it never gets tired.
- **The bot misses** intent, architectural fit, timing/ordering bugs, and anything that requires reading two files together. Everything worth an actual conversation.

I've stopped writing comments the bot would write. If a `null` check is missing and I can see the bot is already commenting, I don't pile on. My review is for the things the bot cannot see.

Net effect: my reviews have gotten *shorter* and *higher-leverage*. Not fewer PRs reviewed — better comments on each.

## Metrics that matter (and that don't)

I track a few things about my own reviewing:

- **Time-to-first-review after request.** Aim for &lt;4h during working hours. This one is on me — reviewer latency is the biggest driver of team cycle time.
- **Approve rate on first pass.** Around 55% for me. Higher would mean I'm reviewing too permissively. Lower would mean I'm asking for changes that could have been discussed before the PR opened.
- **PRs I re-request review on after changes.** Should be ~100%. Otherwise I'm doing drive-by reviews.

Metrics I've explicitly stopped caring about:

- **Comments per review.** Encourages nit-picking.
- **Approval rate.** Rewards rubber-stamping.
- **Reviewer count.** Only matters that the *right* people looked.

## What I ask other reviewers to do

When I onboard someone into reviewing my team's code, three asks:

1. **Assume the author is smart and had context you don't have.** Start comments with a question, not a verdict.
2. **Approve when you'd let it ship.** Not "approve when it's perfect." Perfect never happens; conditional-approvals live in the description.
3. **Reply within one working day.** If you can't, decline the review — don't sit on it.

Those three alone move a team's cycle time more than any process change.

## Closing

Reviewing more PRs than I author isn't a badge. It's a lever. Each review touches someone else's code and — if done well — leaves the codebase and the author both slightly better. Do that a few hundred times a year and the compounding is real.

The signal I trust most that I'm reviewing well: my teammates ship faster after I've reviewed their code, and they ask me to review their next PR. Everything else is vanity.
