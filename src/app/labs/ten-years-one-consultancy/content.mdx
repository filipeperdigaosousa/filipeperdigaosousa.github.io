## The setup

Same employer since the end of 2016. Underneath that constant, I've worked at three different companies as a full-time engineer — each for years, not months.

**WeChangers** (2017–2021) — a social-impact platform. **Pasabi** (2022–2024) — a fraud-detection product. **PlayerData** (2024–present) — a sports performance product. Different tech stacks, different domains, different customers.

That's the whole surface of it. The mechanics of how the employment is structured aren't the interesting part — every arrangement of this shape looks slightly different. What matters is what it does to a career.

## Why it worked for me

Three reasons, none of them particularly noble.

**Variety without job-hunting overhead.** Every 2–4 years I get a completely new problem space — new codebase, new team, new customers — without the reset that a job change normally imposes. Interviews, negotiation, benefits paperwork, re-earning trust with a new manager: I skip all of it. The switching cost is close to zero.

**Continuity of colleagues.** There is a set of people who have watched me across every engagement. When I made a call at a client that turned out great, someone remembers. When I made one that turned out badly, same. That kind of longitudinal feedback is hard to replicate.

**Detachment from any one product's fate.** If a client engagement went sideways tomorrow — layoffs, product cancellation, culture rot — my life doesn't get disrupted. That mental safety net changed how I engage with client work. I ask harder questions. I say uncomfortable things earlier. My best contributions have come out of that latitude.

## Why it isn't for everyone

**You're not fully inside the client's culture.** No equity. No "we" quite the same way. When the client throws a party, you're invited but you didn't build the party. That distance is either a feature (clean-headed judgment) or a bug (less emotional stake), depending on your temperament. For me it turned out to be a feature.

**Loyalty splits.** You owe your best work to the client and your career to the employer. Usually those align. Occasionally they don't. Navigating the small tensions between what a client wants right now and what serves you over years is a skill you only get through practice.

## What it looks like on paper

Recruiters see three things and can't always parse them cleanly:

- "Employer · Dec 2016 – Present · 9+ years"
- "PlayerData · 2024 – Present"
- "Pasabi · 2022 – 2024"
- "WeChangers · 2017 – 2021"

The overlap trips them up. Some read it as job-hopping (missing the tenure). Some read it as flat tenure (missing the client variety). The right read is: **one long employment, three deep client engagements**. My CV now says that explicitly, because leaving it to interpretation was a mistake.

## What I actually learned

Twelve years in, my strongest professional skills are not any particular framework. They are:

**Onboarding into a new codebase in a week.** Three engagements taught me the shape. First day: read the deploy pipeline. Second day: open the biggest recent PR and read every line. Third day: pair with the most senior person on their current work. Fourth day: open a small PR. Fifth day: read the tests directory.

**Reading a team's culture from the artefacts.** How they name branches. How verbose their PR descriptions are. Whether they resolve conversations or leave them open. Whether their `#eng` Slack is loud or quiet. All of that tells you what to bring — assertive or careful, terse or explanatory, structural or tactical.

**Detaching outcomes from ego.** When you might be somewhere else in two years, you can't build for your own legacy. You have to build for the next engineer. That frame is now default for me. It's better for the code.

**Knowing when to say no.** You don't have to inherit every internal fight at a client. You get to be politely tangential to things that aren't your battle. This is a superpower for staying focused. It's also occasionally a moral hazard — sometimes the fight is worth having and you happen to be the best-positioned person to have it. Telling the two apart took years.

## Would I recommend this shape

To some people, wholeheartedly. To others, no.

**Yes** if: you like variety, you don't need founder upside, you value continuity of colleagues over continuity of product, you want deep multi-year engagements not short gigs, you like the "guest at the party" energy of being adjacent rather than embedded.

**No** if: you want to build a company, you want peers who watched you grow from junior at a single product, you want to be *the* person for one thing, you get emotional stake from feeling fully inside a company.

I sit firmly in the first bucket. I've never wanted to found anything. I've always wanted three interesting problems to think about at a time, one strong group of colleagues over years, and enough distance to say what I actually think.

## Closing

Ten years, one employer, three clients. Odd CV shape. Also, on reflection, the shape I would have chosen if I'd known this path was available. Recommend it more than the trade-offs suggest I should.
